Introduction

The 1912 song “He’d Have to Get Under—Get Out and Get Under” describes the the comic trials and tribulations of a young Johnny O’Connor who hopes to canoodle with his sweetheart and marry into millions. However, the pleasures of the Sunday drive are constantly being interrupted by the mechanical failures of his new automobile. This paper argues that Johnny’s repeated frustration is represented in the musical writing on a motivic level. Maurice Abrahams’ music gives thematic shape to the lyrics as the relatively simple theme of Johhny’s sweetheart is made more and more complex and in once instance is used to represent the car.

Leitmotifs

There are two motifs which concern us in this analysis: A chromatic and a bit whimsical melody associated with Johnny which first appears in measures 13-14:

and a diatonic three-note pattern which first appears in measures 17-18. This motif is initially associated with his sweetheart.

These two motifs share a musical period of eight bars which is repeated several times. It is possible the woman’s part in the story happens to fall on this second melody because the song is about Johhny. Structurally, he would get the first four bars and other matters would get the second. However, the significance of the three-note theme is made apparent by the way that it is mutated in the succeeding sections.

Sequential Frustration

In the eight measures leading up to the chorus the three-note diatonic motif speaks out of turn and in rising sequence.

Here the theme of his sweetheart appears transposed and is used instead in the context of the auto. Not the pleasure Johnny is hoping for, this mutated motif interrupts his theme and rises in pitch to heighten tensions before the chorus in which Johnny must get out and get under.

Chromatic Frustration

We see a further frustration in measures 57-60. The three note motif to go with the lyrics “But every minute, when he’d begin it” is a chromatic elaboration of a diatonic transposition of the motif.

The F# tucked into the melody and the diminished fourth which it creates complicate the motif. Here the changes do not represent the car, but simply mirror Johnny’s frustrations on the Sunday drive.
